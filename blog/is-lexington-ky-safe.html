Is Lexington KY Safe? Crime Data & What You Need to Know

An honest, data-informed look at crime and safety in Lexington, Kentucky. What the numbers say, where the risks are, and how to stay informed.

If you're considering a move to Lexington, Kentucky — or you're already here and wondering how your city stacks up — the question "Is Lexington KY safe?" is worth answering with real data rather than assumptions. The short answer: Lexington is safer than many comparable U.S. cities, but like any urban area, crime varies dramatically depending on where you are.

Lexington vs. National Averages

Lexington-Fayette County is a merged city-county government with a population of roughly 320,000, making it Kentucky's second-largest city. When compared to national averages, Lexington's overall crime rate falls in the middle of the pack for cities its size. Violent crime — including assault, robbery, and shooting — tends to be lower than many peer cities in the Southeast and Midwest. Property crime, including theft, burglary, and vandalism, represents the majority of reported incidents, which is consistent with national patterns.

Compared to nearby cities like Louisville, Cincinnati, and Nashville, Lexington generally reports lower rates of violent crime per capita. However, it's important to note that city-wide averages can be misleading because they smooth over the dramatic differences between neighborhoods.

Crime Varies by Neighborhood — A Lot

This is the most important thing to understand about safety in Lexington: your experience depends heavily on your neighborhood. Suburban areas like Andover, Hamburg, and Masterson Station have very low crime rates — often comparable to small-town America. Meanwhile, some areas closer to the urban core see higher concentrations of certain crime types.

Understanding the 9 Crime Categories

SpotCrime tracks nine categories of crime that you'll find on our Lexington maps and reports:

  • Theft — the most commonly reported crime type in Lexington
  • Burglary — unlawful entry into a structure
  • Robbery — theft involving force or threat
  • Assault — physical attacks or threats of violence
  • Shooting — firearm-related incidents
  • Arson — intentional fire-setting
  • Vandalism — property damage and destruction
  • Arrest — documented arrests by law enforcement
  • Other — miscellaneous incidents

Understanding what types of crime are most common in an area helps you assess your actual risk. A neighborhood with occasional theft reports has a very different safety profile than one with frequent assaults.

What Makes Lexington Safer Than Average

Several factors work in Lexington's favor when it comes to safety. The city benefits from a strong local economy anchored by the University of Kentucky, the horse industry, and a growing tech and healthcare sector. Economic stability generally correlates with lower crime rates. Additionally, Lexington's merged city-county government means a unified police department covering the entire Fayette County area, avoiding the jurisdictional gaps that can plague other metro areas.

The Lexington Police Department also publishes crime data publicly, which helps with transparency and community awareness. Active neighborhood associations across the city contribute to a culture of vigilance and mutual support.

Areas to Be More Cautious

While we don't label any neighborhood as "dangerous" — because crime can happen anywhere and conditions change — certain areas of Lexington do report higher incident densities. Some corridors near downtown and along major commercial strips see more property crime. Areas with higher population density and nightlife activity naturally generate more reports.

The best approach is to check real data rather than rely on reputation. Our neighborhood guides and crime map provide the most current picture.

The Bottom Line

Is Lexington KY safe? For most residents, yes — especially if you choose your neighborhood wisely and stay aware of your surroundings. Lexington offers a quality of life that combines affordability, culture, and community in a way that many larger cities can't match.
